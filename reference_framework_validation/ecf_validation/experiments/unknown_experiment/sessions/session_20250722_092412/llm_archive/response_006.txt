**Ranking by Salience:**

1.  **Enmity:** The central theme and driving force of the speech is the condemnation of the President's actions, described in terms of "appalling abuse" and "flagrant assault." This strong negative framing is the core justification for Romney's vote and is rhetorically emphasized throughout.
2.  **Fear:** Closely intertwined with Enmity, the fear dimension underscores the gravity of the situation (threat to institutions, democratic rights) and the personal cost/risk associated with the speaker's decision. This sense of high stakes and duty in the face of perceived threat is pervasive.
3.  **Amity:** Despite the conflict inherent in his decision, Romney explicitly and repeatedly attempts to cultivate a climate of respect and shared good faith among his colleagues. This deliberate effort to maintain collegial bonds, even in profound disagreement, is a prominent rhetorical feature.
4.  **Hope:** Hope serves as an underlying moral foundation for the speech, primarily focused on the enduring strength of the Constitution and the importance of legacy. While not as emotionally vibrant as Enmity or Fear, it provides the ultimate purpose for undertaking his difficult duty.
5.  **Compersion:** Present only as a general affirmation of national values and historical success, it does not actively shape the immediate emotional atmosphere of the speech, which is decidedly somber and duty-oriented.
6.  **Envy:** This emotional climate is virtually non-existent. Any mention of perceived unfair advantage is quickly reframed as an observation about the President's actions rather than an expression of the speaker's own grievance or resentment.

**Overall Emotional Climate Type and Intensity Patterns:**

The overall emotional climate of Mitt Romney's Senate floor speech is one of **high-intensity principled conflict and existential concern, carefully modulated by a deliberate appeal to collegial amity.**

The dominant emotional currents are **Enmity and Fear**, reflecting the profound gravity of the President's alleged actions and the perceived threat they pose to core democratic values and institutions. Romney articulates these threats with strong, direct language ("appalling abuse," "flagrant assault"), indicating a highly charged atmosphere of moral condemnation and alarm. This is compounded by his palpable apprehension about the personal and political consequences of his decision ("awful obligation," "history's rebuke," "abuse from the president").

However, a distinctive feature of this climate is the conscious effort to project **Amity**. Despite delivering a verdict that runs counter to his party and the President, Romney consistently frames his disagreement within a context of mutual respect, shared love for country, and adherence to individual conscience among his fellow senators. This creates a complex emotional tapestry where strong condemnation of actions coexists with an overt desire to maintain professional and personal civility.

**Hope** serves as a quieter, foundational emotion, underpinning the speech with a sense of enduring constitutional principles and the importance of legacy. It is a solemn hope for the nation's long-term integrity rather than immediate optimism. **Envy** and **Compersion** are largely absent, signaling that the speech is not driven by themes of social resentment or celebratory achievement, but solely by a profound sense of duty and the constitutional stakes involved. The intensity is concentrated in the dimensions of conflict and threat, with a deliberate counter-balancing effort in the relational domain.
