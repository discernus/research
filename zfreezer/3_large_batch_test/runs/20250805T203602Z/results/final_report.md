### üî¨ FRAMEWORK OVERVIEW

The Populist Discourse Analysis Framework (PDAF) v7.3 is employed for a comprehensive, cross-ideological evaluation of populist rhetorical patterns. It systematically assesses nine core dimensions, categorized into Primary Populist Core Anchors, Populist Mechanism Anchors, and Boundary Distinction Anchors. The framework utilizes an extended 0.0-2.0 scale to accommodate high-intensity rhetoric and incorporates "populist strategic tension analysis" to measure internal contradictions within populist appeals. Key calculated metrics include the Populist Strategic Contradiction Index (PSCI) and various composite indices such as the Overall Populism Index. The framework's design aims for high inter-rater reliability through clear definitions, sequential analysis, and explicit tension quantification, grounded in established populism theory and empirical measurement.

### üöÄ EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This analysis of American presidential discourse from 1992-2025 reveals a complex landscape of populist rhetoric, with notable variations across presidencies and document types. While the overall trend suggests an increasing presence of populist elements, the statistical analysis indicates a lack of clear temporal shift and significant partisan asymmetry as hypothesized. Specifically, the average populist scores for Democrats and Republicans were nearly identical, and party platforms did not exhibit the predicted moderation compared to candidate speeches. Furthermore, the hypothesized decoupling of populism and nationalism, and consistency of presidential rhetorical signatures, could not be statistically confirmed due to incomplete hypothesis testing.

However, the curated evidence provides qualitative support for the presence of key populist dimensions. For instance, "Manichaean People-Elite Framing" is evident in Democratic platform language contrasting the "American people" with self-serving figures [2], and Republican rhetoric emphasizing an "establishment" that "protected itself, but not the citizens" [1]. "Crisis-Restoration Narratives" are observed across administrations, such as Clinton's call for renewal after past failures [8] and the Republican platform's articulation of an at-risk American Dream [3]. The framework successfully extracted scores for all dimensions, demonstrating its operational capability, but the broader experimental hypotheses could not be fully validated due to statistical limitations in the provided results.
