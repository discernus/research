## üèõÔ∏è Framework Overview

The Civic Analysis Framework (CAF) v7.3 is designed to evaluate the civic character of political discourse by assessing the tension between fundamental civic virtues and their pathological counterparts across five bipolar axes: Dignity‚ÜîTribalism, Truth‚ÜîManipulation, Justice‚ÜîResentment, Hope‚ÜîFear, and Pragmatism‚ÜîFantasy. It employs a sequential, chain-of-thought methodology to score each dimension on a 0.0-1.0 scale, with associated salience and confidence ratings.

Key metrics include **Tension Scores** (e.g., Dignity-Tribalism Tension), a composite **Civic Character Index** (CCI), and a **Salience-Weighted Civic Character Index**. The framework also classifies discourse into **Civic Character Profiles** (High, Mixed, Low, Pathological) and **Strategic Patterns** (e.g., Virtue Signaling, Strategic Pathology). This research aims to differentiate speakers based on these dimensions and identify unique "character signatures."

## üìä EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This analysis applied the Civic Analysis Framework (CAF) v7.3 to a corpus of eight political speeches, aiming to differentiate speakers through their civic character profiles and identify unique "character signatures." The framework assessed discourse across five virtue-pathology dimensions. Notably, Speaker Steve King exhibited the lowest overall Civic Character Index (CCI) at 0.385, characterized by low scores across most virtues and high scores in manipulation and fantasy. In contrast, Senator Cory Booker presented the highest CCI at 0.720, demonstrating a strong inclination towards dignity, truth, justice, and hope.

The study revealed significant differences in how speakers engage with civic virtues. Progressives, on average, scored higher on the Virtue Index (0.74 vs. 0.53 for Conservatives), particularly in hope and justice. Institutional-era politicians generally displayed higher CCI scores (0.715) than Populist-era politicians (0.578). While statistical tests for speaker differentiation were inconclusive due to the single-document-per-speaker design, comparative scores highlight distinct patterns. For instance, John Lewis scored exceptionally high on hope (0.75) and justice (0.85), reflecting his role in the Civil Rights Movement. This suggests the CAF can identify salient civic dimensions for individual speakers, even if broad statistical significance across the entire corpus is limited by sample structure.
